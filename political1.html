Class 12 Political Science Part 1 Detailed Notes (NCERT)

Chapter 1: Cold War Era

Key Themes

  • The Cold War: The Cold War was a period of intense rivalry and ideological conflict between the Soviet Union and the United States from the end of WWII (1945) until the early 1990s. It was marked by nuclear arms races, proxy wars, and competition for global influence without direct military confrontation between the superpowers.
  • Origins of the Cold War: The Cold War began due to the ideological differences between the USSR (communism) and the USA (capitalism). After WWII, Europe was divided into communist East and capitalist West. Tensions arose over the spread of communism and the division of Germany, especially Berlin, leading to the Berlin Blockade (1948-49).
  • The Major Events of the Cold War: Key events include:
    • The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962): A 13-day confrontation between the USA and the USSR over Soviet missiles in Cuba, bringing the world to the brink of nuclear war.
    • The Korean War (1950-1953): A conflict between North Korea (backed by China and the USSR) and South Korea (backed by the US and its allies), which ended in a stalemate and the division of Korea.
    • The Vietnam War (1955-1975): The USA's attempt to stop the spread of communism in South Vietnam, ending in a North Vietnamese victory.
    • The Afghan-Soviet War (1979-1989): The Soviet Union's failed intervention in Afghanistan, which drained Soviet resources and contributed to its eventual collapse.
  • The Role of the United Nations: The UN acted as a mediator during the Cold War, often dealing with proxy conflicts and trying to prevent full-scale wars. However, it was largely ineffective in resolving major Cold War conflicts due to the veto power of the superpowers in the Security Council.
  • Impact on the Global South: The Cold War shaped the political landscape of many newly-independent nations in Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Both the USA and USSR sought to gain influence in these regions through economic aid, military alliances, and support for local regimes or revolutionary movements.

Important Concepts

  • Superpowers: The USA and USSR emerged as superpowers after WWII, dominating global politics and influencing military, economic, and cultural policies worldwide.
  • Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD): The doctrine that ensured neither the USA nor USSR would use nuclear weapons, as both had the capacity to destroy each other in retaliation.
  • Military Alliances: NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization) and the Warsaw Pact were two significant military alliances that divided the world into opposing blocs.

Chapter 2: The End of Bipolarity

Key Themes

  • The Fall of the Soviet Union (1991): A combination of economic stagnation, political reforms (Glasnost and Perestroika), and nationalistic movements within Soviet republics led to the dissolution of the USSR. This marked the end of the Cold War and the beginning of the unipolar world order with the USA as the dominant superpower.
  • Unipolarity: After the fall of the USSR, the USA became the sole superpower. This led to the spread of liberal democracy and free-market capitalism globally, with the USA playing a central role in shaping international politics.
  • The New World Order: A shift from a bipolar to a unipolar world in which the USA dominated global politics. It promoted global trade liberalization, human rights, and the spread of democratic values, though often criticized for unilateral actions, such as the invasion of Iraq (2003).
  • Regional Conflicts: The collapse of bipolarity did not eliminate conflicts. Notable conflicts in the post-Cold War era include:
    • The Gulf War (1990-91): A US-led coalition’s military action to expel Iraqi forces from Kuwait.
    • Ethnic Conflicts in the Balkans: The breakup of Yugoslavia led to violent ethnic conflicts, particularly in Bosnia and Kosovo.
    • The War in Afghanistan (2001-present): The US-led invasion of Afghanistan in response to the 9/11 attacks and the Taliban regime’s support for al-Qaeda.

Important Concepts

  • The US’s Role in Global Politics: The USA’s global influence grew in the post-Cold War period, marked by its military interventions, economic policies (like the promotion of the IMF and WTO), and its leadership in international organizations.
  • Globalization: The growing interconnectedness of the world through trade, technology, and culture, leading to the integration of global markets but also exacerbating inequalities.
  • The Rise of China: China’s rapid economic growth since the 1980s and its increasing influence in world politics and economics, challenging the dominance of the US in various spheres.

Chapter 3: US Hegemony in World Politics

Key Themes

  • The USA as a Global Hegemon: After WWII, the USA emerged as the dominant global power, not just militarily but economically and culturally. The USA led efforts to create international institutions like the United Nations, the World Bank, and the IMF to stabilize the global order.
  • US Military and Economic Power: The USA used its military might (e.g., through NATO) and economic influence (e.g., through trade agreements, sanctions, and the IMF) to assert its dominance globally. The use of military force in countries like Iraq and Afghanistan, as well as economic tools like sanctions on Iran, reflects its hegemony.
  • American Cultural Influence: American culture has spread globally through media, music, Hollywood films, and technology. The concept of the "American Dream" became a symbol of aspiration worldwide.
  • The War on Terror (2001–present): The US-led global campaign against terrorism, following the 9/11 attacks, led to military interventions in Afghanistan and Iraq and a global expansion of counterterrorism measures.

Important Concepts

  • Unilateralism vs. Multilateralism: The debate over whether the USA should act alone (unilateralism) or cooperate with other countries and international organizations (multilateralism) to solve global problems.
  • Economic Sanctions: The use of economic measures to pressure countries, like North Korea or Iran, to comply with international norms or demands. These are often used by the USA to enforce foreign policy.
  • Global Trade and the IMF: The USA’s dominant role in shaping global economic policy through institutions like the IMF and WTO, often pushing for market liberalization and free trade.

Chapter 4: Alternative Centres of Power

Key Themes

  • The Rise of China: China's rapid economic growth and its political and military assertiveness have made it a significant global player. Its rise is challenging US dominance, particularly in Asia and Africa.
  • The European Union (EU): The EU represents a unique form of regional integration that has played an important role in global politics, particularly through its economic power and political influence in multilateral organizations.
  • Emerging Economies – BRICS: The BRICS nations (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) are a group of rapidly developing economies that are reshaping global power dynamics, advocating for a more multipolar world order.

Important Concepts

  • Multipolarity: A world order where power is distributed among multiple states, rather than being dominated by a single superpower. This is evident in the rise of countries like China and India.
  • Soft Power: The ability of a country to influence others through cultural appeal, diplomacy, and economic aid, as demonstrated by China’s use of its economic growth and strategic investments worldwide.
  • Regionalism and Regional Organizations: The rise of regional organizations like ASEAN, the African Union, and Mercosur highlights the importance of regional cooperation in global governance.

Chapter 5: Contemporary South Asia

Key Themes

  • India-Pakistan Relations: The historical and ongoing tensions between India and Pakistan, primarily due to the Kashmir conflict, nuclear weapons, and cross-border terrorism.
  • Bangladesh’s Struggle for Independence: The political and military events leading to the 1971 liberation of Bangladesh from Pakistan, and its aftermath.
  • Ethnic Conflicts in Sri Lanka: The Tamil insurgency, civil war, and the eventual defeat of the LTTE (Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam) by the Sri Lankan government.
  • The Role of the SAARC: The South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (SAARC) and its role in fostering economic cooperation and resolving conflicts within South Asia.

Important Concepts

  • Nuclearization of South Asia: The arms race between India and Pakistan, and the nuclear tests conducted by both countries, significantly influencing regional security.
  • Democratic vs. Authoritarian Regimes: The varying political systems within South Asia, with India being the world’s largest democracy, while Pakistan, Sri Lanka, and Bangladesh have seen both democratic and military regimes.

Chapter 6: International Organizations

Key Themes

  • The United Nations (UN): The formation of the UN post-WWII to maintain international peace and security, promote human rights, and foster cooperation on economic and social issues.
  • International Monetary Fund (IMF) and World Bank: Their roles in providing financial aid and loans to developing countries, and the conditions attached to such assistance.
  • World Trade Organization (WTO): The role of the WTO in regulating global trade, setting trade rules, and resolving disputes between member countries.

Important Concepts

  • International Cooperation: The growing importance of international organizations in addressing global issues like climate change, terrorism, and poverty.
  • Reform of the UN: Debates on reforming the UN, particularly the Security Council, to reflect the changing geopolitical realities and ensure better representation of emerging powers like India and Brazil.
